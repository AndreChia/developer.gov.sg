Overview

The close integration of Design, Product and Engineering is critical for agile projects to succeed.

The project owner/product owner is committed to user centred practices and continuous iteration based on user research, prototyping, testing with real users.

The collaboration model should be that Design, Product & Engineering work as equals versus one reporting into the other.

There is the commitment to resource designers, and design as a continuous work stream, throughout the entire project lifecycle.

There are three key phases of design throughout the digital project lifecycle:

  • Discovery
  • Product Conceptualisation
  • UX Design

Discovery

What is the Discovery Phase about?

The purpose of discovery is to identify and understand the problem that needs to be solved, before committing to building a digital product or service. This means learning about:

  • Potential users and what they are trying to achieve
  • Any operations or technical constraints (e.g. existing processes, systems, legacy technology)
  • The underlying policy intent that the government wants to implement
  • Opportunities areas to create improvement or enable change

What we learn through discovery will guide whether to move forward to product conceptualisation. It should not be an exercise to simply validate customer hypotheses or requirements.

Example activities during discovery include user research, mapping and prioritising pain points, framing problem statements and opportunities, testing hypotheses, exploring alternatives to building a new digital product or service that could solve the problem more effectively.

It is not a failure if the project does not proceed to product conceptualisation - if the research shows that the problem is better addressed via alternative solutions.

How long should a Discovery Phase take?

There’s no set time period for discovery, but around 8 to 12 weeks is typical. Let the purpose of your discovery dictate how long the team spends on it.

If you’re working on a problem that no one has researched before, you might need a bit longer. If it’s a problem and group of users that you know a fair bit about already, you might be able to get away with a slightly shorter discovery.

Deciding whether to move forward to Product Conceptualisation

Do not proceed to product conceptualisation if:

There is no compelling problem, opportunity or user need
There is no compelling argument for the Government to provide the digital product or service
User needs are already being well met by existing solutions
Tech or policy constraints mean solution cannot be delivered anytime soon
Partner stakeholders are not ready for user-centred practices

If you do decide to move on to product conceptualisation, you’ll need to make sure you:

Understand the wider context and overall service journey, and the other teams and organisations that could be working to address similar and related problems
Are clear on how what you’re working on fits into that wider problem space and service journey
Have a list of possible concepts that you’d like to test during product conceptualisation and an idea of which one you’d like to prototype first (and why)
