Discovery

The purpose of discovery is to identify and understand the problem that needs to be solved, before committing to building a digital product or service. This means learning about:

  1. Potential users and what they are trying to achieve

  2. Any operations or technical constraints (e.g. existing processes, systems, legacy technology)

  3. The underlying policy intent that the government wants to implement

  4. Opportunities areas to create improvement or enable change

What is gathered during discovery will help determine whether to move forward to product conceptualisation.

Note that developing a hypothesis and other fundamental research questions that define the identified problem is an important phase here. Strong interest by the public and key stakeholders in finding out answers to these questions will indicate the urgency and relevance of the problem.

User research is crucial in this process, as it enables the team to understand their users’ lifestyles, habits and how they use or experience existing services, which results in a creation of a service that fits the users’ needs.

Understand that it is not a failure if the project does not proceed to product conceptualisation - if the research shows that the problem is better addressed via alternative solutions.

Length of discovery

There’s no set time period for discovery, but around 8 to 12 weeks is typical. Let the purpose and process of discovery dictate how long the team spends on it. It may take a little longer if the team is working on a problem that no one has researched before. If the problem and group of users is already well understood, the team might be able to get away with a slightly shorter discovery.

Do not proceed to product conceptualisation if:

  • There is no compelling problem, opportunity or user need

  • There is no compelling argument for the Government to provide the digital product or service

  • User needs are already being well met by existing solutions

  • Tech or policy constraints mean solution cannot be delivered anytime soon

  • Partner stakeholders are not ready for user-centred practices

If moving on to product conceptualisation, teams must:

  • Understand the wider network context, or the other teams and organisations that could be working to address similar and related problems

  • Have a list of possible concepts to test during product conceptualisation and an idea of which one to prototype first (and why)

  • Understand the reason for using technology as a solution. Many problems do not actually require technological solutions and many technological solutions need significant support from non-technological solutions (e.g. non-optimal business processes). Make sure that all stakeholders understand this.

The further along a project is in terms of development, the more funding and KPIs will be locked in. Hence, it is essential that the proper due diligence is done during this problem validation phase to ensure that the project is a worthwhile one.
