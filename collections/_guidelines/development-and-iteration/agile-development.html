The values and principles of agile development come from the Agile Manifesto. It encourages rapid iterative development with the objective of bringing flexibility and speed for projects and products. It promotes frequent adaptation and inspection which results in rapid delivery of high-quality solutions while constantly aligning product development with user requirements.

Scrum

At GovTech, cross-functional teams use Scrum to work together effectively. Scrum is a framework that guides teams in implementing an Agile way of working. It is based on empiricism, which states that decisions should be made based on what is known and what is known comes from experience and observation. This is the basis for the iterative and incremental nature of running digital projects - so that decisions can be based on results, rather than speculation or guesswork. For Scrum to work, there must be transparency, inspection and adaptation.

A scrum team consists of a product owner, a scrum master and the development team. The scrum team is self-organising and self-managing, which means they decide how best to deliver their work, rather than being directed by someone outside the team. They are also cross-functional – all the skills or competencies required to do the work are within the team.

Product Owner

The product owner should be someone with vision, authority and availability. The product owner needs to have a vision for how the product should develop and the authority to make decisions. They are also responsible for communicating the projects’ vision and priorities to the development team.

The product owner ensures the team delivers maximum business value by prioritising and managing the product backlog.

The product owner is responsible for:

  • Writing the product backlog items clearly

  • Prioritising the product backlog items to achieve the most value and to meet the team’s goals

  • Making sure the product backlog is visible, transparent, shows the team what to work on next and that the team understands the items

  • If the product owner is to succeed, the organisation needs to respect their decisions and anyone wanting to change the priority of a product backlog item must go through the product owner. No one else should give the development team a different set of requirements.

The Scrum Master

The scrum master ensures that Scrum is understood and practised by the product owner, the development team and the organisation. The role of scrum master does not have to be fixed - anyone in the development team can take up this role. This also highlights the importance of scrum being practised by every member of the team instead of being only a responsibility of the scrum master.

The scrum master does this by ensuring the scrum team follows Scrum theory, practices and rules, and removing any obstacles the team encounters.

The scrum master is responsible for:

  • Facilitating daily scrum, sprint planning, sprint demo and retrospective meetings

  • Forecasting the numbers of deliverables possible in an iteration, which is based on evidence and reliable source

  • Creating useful, reliable and practical plans for software development projects

  • Tracking and removing project impediments

Product Backlog

A product backlog is a living artefact and is never complete or finished. It evolves as the product and the environment change to ensure that the product is always appropriate, competitive and useful.

The product owner is responsible for maintaining the product backlog and arranging the items in order of priority.

Usually, the items at the top of the product backlog are more detailed than the ones lower on the list. The product owner will work with the development team to add detail to the backlog items during product backlog refinement. Only items for the next 2–3 sprints are defined in greater detail. Items further down the list are expected to change by the time the team gets to them, so we don’t waste time refining them until we’re ready to work on them.
