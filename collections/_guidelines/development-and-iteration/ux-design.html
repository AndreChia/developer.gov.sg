The purpose of user experience design is to take responsibility for all aspects of the end-users’ interaction with government services and its associated digital experiences. It starts with the gathering of user insights, its translation into the design and implementation of user interactions, content, interfaces and processes, but can stretch to reimagining entire service journeys to make them simpler, more human-centred and more impactful. This means:

  • Designing how people will interact with a digital product or service, and how to elicit the desired user behaviours and outcomes

  • Defining how information should be structured, the relationships between different types of content and making it easy to navigate

  • Defining how the presentation layers of a product or service look, to elevate the user experience, usability and engagement

  • Running usability testing to identify friction, inform design iteration and qualify improvements

  • Making sure everyone can use the digital service by testing for accessibility and addressing usability challenges that might lead to people being unintentionally excluded

UX design is about supporting the agile development of the digital product or service in a sustainable way, and continuing to iterate and make improvements informed by user research, testing and product data. It should not be an exercise to simply deliver on user stories.

Example activities include close collaboration with product owners, product managers, software engineers, quality engineers, etc., delivery of low-fidelity to high-fidelity design assets as appropriate throughout the agile sprint cycle and balancing the short term need to deliver on the product backlog with the need to look ahead at how user needs are changing and how the user experience needs to evolve to meet those changing needs.

Throughout this process, it is important to allow the team to self-organise and take autonomy in trying out new roles, methods and resources they perceive will improve efficiency and collaboration. Some methods to cultivate this includes: 

  • Including the team in all stages of a project life cycle. This enables the team to understand the product’s start-to-finish context to drive more targeted solutions and enhance a sense of accountability. 

  • Encouraging team members to offer suggestions to each other, even in areas that are not their specialisation (e.g. an engineer giving ideas to a UX designer). This can spur fresh, innovative and interdisciplinary eureka moments and solutions.

At the same time, it is key to encourage the team to be unafraid to fail. Failures often reveal key mistakes and motivates the team to learn and re-learn. Often, it is only through rock-bottom scenarios that provide important lessons that the project can head north again. 

Product humility is another important virtue for success. Team members should be reminded to refrain from perceiving their product or idea as the best, or the only feasible solution. Openness to fresh ideas and implementation methods is key for improvement.
